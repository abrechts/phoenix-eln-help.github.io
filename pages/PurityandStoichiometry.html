Purity and Stoichiometry

Yield Calculations

The reaction yield often is a first sight quality indicator of a reaction - the higher, the better. However, if purity is not part of the game (i.e. assumed to be 100%), this becomes an ambiguous assessment. Thus, it should be obvious that a 95% percent yield of a crude product with a purity of just 25% is not the same as a 95% yield of the same product of 97% purity - although the yields look identical. In the worst case, this can be misleading.

Therefore stoichiometric yield calculations usually relate available product mols to available reactant mols. Available mols are not only determined by material amount and molecular weight, but also by purity. Thus, a given amount of a material of 50% purity actually contains only half the mols of the same pure material.   

Purity Effect on Yield: 

The reaction yield is increased by decreasing reference reactant purity, and decreased by decreasing reference product purity.

It's ultimately a team decision on how to handle purities for yield calculations. The important thing is to agree on a common policy to obtain comparable yields with a well understood meaning,

Equivalent Calculations

Assigning a reference reactant purity influences the reagent equivalent calculations in analogy to above yield calculations. The lower the reference reactant purity, the lower the calculated reagent amount for a given equivalent specification. If a reagent is specified as weight or volume, then its calculated equivalents are increased when the reference reactant purity is reduced. 

Phoenix ELN Purity Support

The material dialogs for reference reactants, reagents and products contain a text box for entering a purity in percent. If a value is specified there, it becomes part of the stoichiometric calculations described above. If left empty, a 100% purity is assumed.
